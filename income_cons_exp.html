A Comparative Time Series Analysis of Income-Consumption Relationships in the US and Japan

View Full Project (PDF)
View Full Project Code (GitHub)

Abstract

This study investigates the dynamics between national income and household consumption in the United States and Japan using annual series from 1975 to 2021. A battery of time-series techniques (unit root testing, Engle–Granger cointegration checks, Vector Autoregression (VAR), Granger causality tests, variance decompositions, and structural break analysis (Chow test)) is applied to uncover both long-run and short-run relationships. Results indicate no robust cointegration for the US series and mismatched integration orders for Japan (income I(1), consumption trend-stationary), preventing cointegration there. VAR-based analyses show no significant Granger causality in either direction; variance decomposition highlights consumption shocks as a dominant source of variance for income, and a structural break is detected in Japan around 1991.

Introduction

The link between income and consumption is a central question in macroeconomics. Differences in saving and consumption behavior across countries—especially between Japan, with historically high household saving rates, and the United States, with lower saving and higher consumption propensities—motivate a comparative time-series analysis. Using annual World Bank indicators (adjusted net national income per capita and household final consumption expenditure in constant 2015 US$), this paper explores whether observed co-movements reflect long-run equilibrium relationships or short-run dynamics.

Literature Review

Foundational contributions from Keynes (Absolute Income Hypothesis) and Friedman (Permanent Income Hypothesis) frame our empirical approach. Empirical studies (e.g., Campbell 1987; King et al. 1991; Lettau & Ludvigson 2003) find mixed evidence for cointegration between consumption and income depending on data frequency, variable definitions, and sample period. This paper situates itself within that literature by offering a long-sample (1975–2021) annual comparison between two structurally different economies.

Data

Annual series for Adjusted net national income per capita (constant 2015 US$) and Household & NPISH final consumption expenditure (constant 2015 US$) are taken from the World Bank World Development Indicators for the United States and Japan, covering 1975–2021.

Descriptive & Visual Inspection

Scatterplots and trends reveal a tighter income–consumption co-movement in the US (R² ≈ 0.993) and a looser relationship in Japan (R² ≈ 0.968). Japan’s series display a flatter trajectory since the early 1990s, motivating a structural break investigation.

Autocorrelations & Unit Root Tests

ACF and PACF plots for levels and first differences guide stationarity decisions. Formal ADF and Phillips–Perron tests indicate:

  • United States: income and consumption are I(1) (non-stationary in levels; stationary in first differences).
  • Japan: income is I(1), but consumption appears trend-stationary (I(0) after detrending), producing mismatched integration orders.

Cointegration Tests

Engle–Granger residual-based tests are applied where appropriate. For the US, both OLS residual inspection and the EG test fail to provide robust evidence of cointegration (EG tau-statistics p-values ≈ 0.25–0.28), implying no stable long-run equilibrium for the chosen annual series. For Japan, cointegration testing is not advisable due to the differing integration orders (income I(1), consumption trend-stationary).

Vector Autoregression (VAR) Analysis

VAR models with two lags (selected by AIC/BIC) are estimated on stationary transformations (first differences or detrended series). The VAR framework facilitates Granger causality testing and variance decomposition to probe short-run interdependencies.

Granger Causality

Granger causality tests detect no statistically significant predictive relationships between income and consumption in either country: past income does not Granger-cause consumption and past consumption does not Granger-cause income at conventional significance levels.

Variance Decomposition

Forecast error variance decompositions (via Cholesky identification) show that consumption variance is mainly explained by its own shocks. Income receives a substantial portion of its variance explained by consumption shocks — approximately 70% for the US and 60% for Japan at relevant horizons — suggesting that the short-run dynamics operate more strongly from consumption to income.

Structural Break Analysis

A Chow test with a breakpoint in 1991 (the onset of Japan's "Lost Decades") is performed to assess parameter stability. All three test statistics (F = 4.19, LR = 8.36, Wald = 8.37) reject the null at the 5% level (p ≈ 0.015–0.022), indicating a structural break in Japan's income–consumption relationship around 1991. This motivates potential split-sample VAR estimations or inclusion of interaction dummies.

Conclusion

The comparative analysis yields three central findings. First, the US annual series do not display robust cointegration in this sample, contrary to some earlier quarterly-based studies. Second, Japan’s consumption and income exhibit different integration properties (precluding standard cointegration tests), and a structural break in 1991 further alters the relationship. Third, VAR-based short-run analyses show a lack of Granger causality between income and consumption in either country, while variance decompositions point to consumption shocks as the major driver of income variance (70% US, 60% Japan). Taken together, these results suggest that the classical Permanent Income Hypothesis provides an incomplete description for annual aggregate behavior in these two economies over 1975–2021.

Extensions: future work could (1) split Japanese data pre/post-1991 or include breakpoint dummies, (2) augment models with wealth and credit indicators, (3) experiment with quarterly data where available, and (4) apply structural VARs to test alternative identifications.
